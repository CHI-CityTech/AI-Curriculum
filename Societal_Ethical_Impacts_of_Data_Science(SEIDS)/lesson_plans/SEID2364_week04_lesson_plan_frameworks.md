# SEID 2364 – Week 4 Lesson Plan
## Topic: From Harm to Frameworks

---

## Provocation (Opening – No Framing)

Students respond privately (5–7 minutes):

1. When no option avoids harm, what does it mean to choose the "right" action?

2. Provide an example in which two people arrive at different—but each defensible—evaluations of harm in the same situation. What leads to those differences?

3. What different ways might people decide between competing harms?

---

## Part 2 – Class Plan (After Provocation)

### 1. Quick Plan Reveal (1–2 min)

After initial responses are surfaced briefly, state the plan for the session:

- We will examine how we are making decisions about harm
- We will check and stabilize our working systems (GitHub + Zotero)
- We will return to our responses and build toward a more structured way of deciding

(Keep this concise—no terminology yet.)

### 3. Return to Provocation Responses (Zoom Whiteboard) – 10–12 min

- Students share responses more fully
- Instructor captures key terms/phrases on Whiteboard

#### Whiteboard Layout (Explicit)

Divide the board into three evolving regions (do not label immediately—allow structure to emerge):

**Left Column – Situations / Examples**
- Draw primarily from student responses to Provocation Question 2
- Each entry should be a brief reference to a student-provided scenario
- e.g., “medical triage,” “financial loss vs injury,” “AI decision”

Instructor note:
You are not introducing new scenarios here—you are extracting and labeling the ones students already generated in their responses

**Center Column – Reasoning / Justifications**
- Why the student chose a particular action
- e.g., “minimize total harm,” “protect individual rights,” “intent matters”

**Right Column – Points of Tension / Conflict**
- Where disagreements appear
- e.g., “individual vs group,” “short-term vs long-term,” “emotional vs physical harm”

Instructor behavior:
- Let categories emerge before naming them
- Reposition items as patterns become clearer
- Draw lines or groupings to show relationships and contradictions

Focus on:
- Different reasoning strategies
- Contradictions
- Ambiguities

---

### 4. Identify Instability – 5–7 min

Instructor guides discussion:

- Different people → different answers
- Same situation → different evaluations

Prompt:
> If two people can justify different decisions in the same situation, what does that suggest about how we are making decisions?

Follow-up:
> What happens if we rely only on individual judgment?

---

### 5. Transition to Structure – 3–5 min

Instructor introduces:

> If we want decisions that are not arbitrary, we need some way to make them more consistent.

---

### 6. Meta-Criteria Development – 10–12 min

Prompt:

> A framework system is useful only if it can…

Students complete the statement.

Instructor captures on Whiteboard.

Likely themes:
- Consistency
- Clarity / Explainability
- Fairness
- Applicability across cases
- Justification

---

### 7. The Assessment Problem – 8–10 min

Shift from generation to evaluation:

> How do we know if a framework actually satisfies these conditions?

Focus on one criterion (e.g., fairness):

> If a framework claims to be “fair,” how would we determine whether it actually is?

Goal:
Move from claims → testable evaluation.

---

### 8. Parameter Identification – 8–10 min

Prompt:

> Fair with respect to what?

#### Whiteboard Extension (Harm Taxonomy Construction)

Extend the existing whiteboard (do not start a new one).

Create a new section (below or adjacent):

**Harm Types (Emergent List)**
- Students propose types of harm based on earlier scenarios
- Instructor captures terms without filtering initially

Examples that may emerge:
- Physical
- Economic
- Psychological
- Social
- Reputational
- Temporal (short vs long-term)

Instructor behavior:
- Do NOT present a predefined list
- Consolidate duplicates (e.g., "emotional" → "psychological")
- Ask for clarification when categories are vague

Key move:
> Where does this harm show up in our earlier scenarios?

Draw connections from harm types back to specific scenarios on the left column.

Optional prompt:
> Are there harms we are missing?

Goal:
Students construct a **working taxonomy of harm** that is:
- incomplete
- debatable
- revisable

Instructor note:
This list becomes the basis for the next assignment (students must select and justify relevant harm types, not enumerate all possible ones).

---

### 9. The Comparison Problem – 8–10 min

Prompt:

> How do we compare different types of harm?

Examples:
- Physical injury vs financial loss
- Individual harm vs collective harm
- Immediate vs delayed harm

Key realization:
- Harms are not directly commensurable
- Comparison requires a system, not intuition

---

### 10. Weighting and Evaluation – 5–7 min

Surface the underlying structure:

Any system that evaluates harm must:
- Identify parameters (what counts)
- Measure or describe magnitude (how much)
- Compare across types
- Determine weighting (what matters more)

Keep conceptual—do not formalize mathematically.

---

### 11. Introduction to Frameworks – 10–12 min

Instructor introduces concept:

> Ethical frameworks are systems that attempt to solve these problems.

Brief overview:
- Consequentialism (outcomes → requires comparison/weighting)
- Deontology (rules → constrains comparison)
- Virtue Ethics (judgment → contextual evaluation)

Keep high-level and explicitly connect back to:
- parameters
- comparison
- weighting

Instructor introduces concept:

> Systems like this already exist. These are called frameworks.

Brief overview:
- Consequentialism (outcomes)
- Deontology (rules)
- Virtue Ethics (judgment/character)

Keep high-level

---

### 9. Optional Application (If Time)

Return to earlier examples

Ask:
- What would a rule-based approach do?
- What would an outcome-based approach do?

Map responses onto Whiteboard

---

### 10. Closing – 3–5 min

Reinforce:
- Different systems produce different answers
- Goal is structured reasoning, not agreement

---

### 11. Assignment Introduction (Preview for Week 5) – 3–5 min

Frame the assignment (no full detail yet):

> You are not choosing a framework—you are testing one.

#### Assignment Overview

Students will:

1. **Select a Scenario**
- Use a scenario from today’s discussion OR a provided case
- Must involve competing harms with no obvious resolution

2. **Identify Types of Harm**
- Select and define relevant harm categories (from our class taxonomy)
- Minimum of 3 types
- Justify why each applies

3. **Apply One Framework**
- Use a single ethical framework to determine a decision

4. **Test the Framework (Weighting Exploration)**
- Propose at least two different ways of prioritizing harms
- Observe whether the outcome changes

5. **Evaluate the Framework**
- Does it:
  - Clearly compare harms?
  - Handle multiple types of harm?
  - Provide a defensible sense of fairness?
  - Remain stable under changing assumptions?

#### Key Idea

> The goal is not to find the “correct” answer, but to examine how a system behaves under pressure.

Instructor note:
- Full assignment details and template will be provided next session
- Students should begin identifying possible scenarios and noting harm types before leaving class
